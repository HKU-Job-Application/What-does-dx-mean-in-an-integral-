The purpose of my explanations is for you to keep in mind what exactly is an integral over an area $C$ or over an interval $I$, and what exactly it means to bring in another measure $\text{d}r\,\text{d}\theta$ or $\text{d}u$ in the middle of evaluating an integral.
It's useful to have these pictures in mind, because when you're jumping from one surface to another, from one variable to another, you can easily get confused and forget to put in a term to correct for the "wrong" measure or change an integral limit.
So, if you ever find yourself having to calculate an integral exactly and you know you've made a mistake somewhere but don't know where, you can step back and think about the geometric picture, one step at a time:
OK, after changing the way in which the function sums things up or multiplies things up, I'm now at the point when I'm bringing in these alternative variables, because it simplifies the integral. In order to check that I haven't messed it up, I need to picture this. What am I trying to achieve? To break up the surface into little pieces, multiply the actual area of each with the function, then add it all up. I'm NOT going to look at $\text{d}A$ and say, "Ooh, the area of a sphere is $4\pi R^2$, so if I expect $\text{d}R$ to appear a few lines later I'll just have $\text{d}A=8\pi R\,\text{d}R$." This isn't what $\text{d}A$ stands for - it stands for little pieces of $A$. But these alternative variables, which I need for the calculation, give me a different measure. They say, "pretend the areas of the little pieces are not measured properly, but by corresponding them to this other thing and finding out what the area is in that other thing". So I have to correct for this wrong area by bringing in the Jacobian. What is the Jacobian? ...

If you've taken high school math, you're familiar with substitution of variables as a technique for integrals. Suppose someone said to you that they would much rather evaluate the integral over \(u\), where \(x = u^2\). With the rules you learnt in high school, you can automatically change the integral limits from \(\displaystyle\int_0^4\) to \(\displaystyle\int_0^2\), since \(4=2^2\); you can automatically change \(e^{\sin(x)}\) to \(e^{\sin(u^2)}\); and you can automatically change \(\text{d}x\) to \(2u\,\text{d}u\).

Here's the question I have for you. Why do we replace \(\text{d}x\) with \(2u\,\text{d}u\)? Why not just have \(\text{d}u\)?

You can perhaps write down what the substitution rule for \(u = x^2\) is, and prove it formally. (In other words, to prove that \(\displaystyle\int_0^{t^2} f(x)\,\text{d}x\) is the same as \(\displaystyle\int_0^t 2u\,f(u^2)\,\text{d}u\), note that LHS and RHS are both zero when \(t\) is zero, and then note that the derivatives of both sides with respect to \(t\) are the same, namely \(2t\,f(t)\). LHS and RHS are thus the same function.)

But I want to explore the geometric explanation for this, which is more enlightening.

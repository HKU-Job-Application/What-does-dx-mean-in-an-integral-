Let me make two points.
First, if the height of each rectangle is the same as its counterpart on the right, what makes the combined area so different? Obviously, the widths. But we can go further and ask, what is the relationship of the width of one rectangle on one side and the width of the counterpart on the other? In other words, what is the relationship between \(x_{i+1}-x_i\) and \(u_{i+1}-u_i\)?
We could try to look at their ratio, \(\dfrac{x_{i+1}-x_i}{u_{i+1}-u_i}\). Now remember, the way we set it up, \(x_i=u_i^2\). So we're really investigating \(\dfrac{u_{i+1}^2-u_i^2}{u_{i+1}-u_i}\).
Doesn't that remind you of some famous expression? Something like \(\dfrac{g(u+\epsilon)-g(u)}{\epsilon}\), where \(g(u)=u^2\)? That's right! When we choose divisions of the interval where the sub-intervals get thinner and thinner, \(\dfrac{u_{i+1}^2-u_i^2}{u_{i+1}-u_i}\) gets closer to the derivative of \(g(u)\) at \(u_i\), in other words \(2u_i\). That's where the rule for substituting variables comes from. The ratio between \(\text{d}x\) and \(\text{d}u\) really is \(2u\).

Notice something interesting. $\sqrt{2}\dfrac{i+\frac{1}{2}}{n}$ is exactly the average of $r_i=\sqrt{2}\dfrac{i}{n}$ and $r_{i+1}=\sqrt{2}\dfrac{i+1}{n}$. Let's rewrite the same expression.
\[ \sum_{0\leq i,j\leq n-1} f(x,y)|_{(x,y)\text{ is some point in the C-piece paired to }R_{i,j}}\,\text{Av}(r_i,r_{i+1})\,(r_{i+1}-r_i)(\theta_{j+1}-\theta_j) \]
Finally, it would be interesting to see what happens, if we want every quantity in the sum to belong to the "pretend" $r\theta$ world. There's no theoretical reason why we must - the above expression is fine. But if we do make that transfer, we can discover why anyone would bother with the $r\theta$ substitution in the first place. It ends up with an expression that is more familiar and easier algebraically.
Expressing these values of $f(x,y)$ (which we are still using) in terms of $r_i$ and $\theta_i$, is helped by the fact that the $f(x,y)$ we're looking at is $\exp(-x^2-y^2)$.
Now, what value does $\exp(-x^2-y^2)$ have, where $(x,y)$ is indeed some point in the C-piece paired to $R_{i,j}$? (Any point in the C-piece is fine, we don't have to worry about this particular point or that; as I've stated, Riemann showed that mathematically it won't make a difference to the final answer as we use better and better divisions of $C$.) Well, $x^2+y^2$ is the square of what the $r$-coordinate would be in the "pretend" $r\theta$ space. (If you've forgotten why, let's remember that $x=r\cos(\theta)$ and $y=r\sin(\theta)$.) Since we're looking at $R_{i,j}$, the $r$-coordinate ranges from $r_i$ to $r_{i+1}$.
So, since any point within the C-piece is okay, let's just take $\exp(-x^2-y^2)$ to be $\exp(-r_i^2)$. We can finally rewrite the sum we want, as
\[ \sum_{0\leq i,j\leq n-1} \exp(-r_i^2)\,\text{Av}(r_i,r_{i+1})\,(r_{i+1}-r_i)(\theta_{j+1}-\theta_j) \]

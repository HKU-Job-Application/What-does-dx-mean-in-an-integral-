We have some division of the interval \([1,\sqrt{3}]\), which we can write \(x_0=1,x_1,x_2,...,x_{n-1},x_n=\sqrt{3}\). We can visualise the approximation of \(\displaystyle\int_1^\sqrt{3} \dfrac{1}{1+x^2}\,\text{d}x\) as \(\displaystyle\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}\dfrac{1}{1+x_i^2}(x_{i+1}-x_i)\). But, for whatever reason, we don't like the sub-intervals \([x_i,x_{i+1}]\). We like the sub-intervals \([u_i,u_{i+1}]\), where \(u_i=\arctan(x_i)\) or equivalently \(x_i=\tan(u_i)\). We want to use \([u_i,u_{i+1}]\) in our summation.
However, we can't just do \(\displaystyle\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}\dfrac{1}{1+x_i^2}(u_{i+1}-u_i)\). We'd almost certainly get the wrong answer from that. Why? Because the length \((u_{i+1}-u_i)\) might not be anywhere similar to \((x_{i+1}-x_i)\). We have to account for the fact that \(\dfrac{x_{i+1}-x_i}{u_{i+1}-u_i}\) is roughly the derivative of \(\tan(x)\) evaluated at \(u_i\), which is \(\sec^2(u_i)\).
So the integral we want is approximated by \(\displaystyle\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}\dfrac{1}{1+x_i^2}\sec^2(u_i)(u_{i+1}-u_i)\). Again, remember that \(x_i=\tan(u_i)\). So we're really trying to compute \(\displaystyle\int_{\pi/4}^{\pi/3} \dfrac{1}{1+\tan^2(u)}\sec^2(u)\,\text{d}u\).
Now, of course, we know that the thing in the integral is just \(1\). So the final answer is \(\dfrac{\pi}{3}-\dfrac{\pi}{4}=\dfrac{\pi}{12}\).

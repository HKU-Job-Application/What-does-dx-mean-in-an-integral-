Just as \(\displaystyle\int_{[a,b]}f(x)\,\text{d}x\) divides the interval into little sub‑intervals, multiplies the normal length of the sub‑interval by some value of the function on that sub‑interval, and then adds up the rectangular areas,
\( f(x) = e^{-x^{2}} \)
so \(\displaystyle\int_C f(x,y)\,\text{d}x\,\text{d}y\) divides \(C\) into little squares, multiplies the normal area of the sub‑square by some value of the function on that sub‑square, and then adds up the volumes of the cuboids.
See below a graph of the surface \(\displaystyle f(x,y)=\exp (-x^2-y^2) \) over the disc \(C = \{x^2+y^2\le 2\}\). You can also see a division of \(C\) into tiny squares of side \(\Delta = 0.2\). If you hover over or click any of these sub‑squares, you can see the volume of the thin cuboid highlighted on the 3‑D graph.

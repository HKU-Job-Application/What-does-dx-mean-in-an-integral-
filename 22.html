What happens when the divisions of the $r\theta$ rectangle get finer and finer? (By the way - writing $\epsilon$ to mean that all sub-shapes in our division has area smaller than $\epsilon$, and writing $\delta$ to mean that all sub-shapes in our division have diameter smaller than $\delta$, the diameter having been defined a few slides ago as the distance between the furthest two points of the sub-shape - if we know that the $\epsilon$ and $\delta$ for the divisions we are using of $C$ go to zero, we can be sure that the $\epsilon$ and $\delta$ for the corresponding divisions of the pretend rectangle also go to zero. And vice versa. This is true mathematically.)
In the expression $\displaystyle\sum_{0\leq i,j\leq n-1} \exp(-r_i^2)\,\text{Av}(r_i,r_{i+1})\,(r_{i+1}-r_i)(\theta_{j+1}-\theta_j)$, because $r_i$ and $r_{i+1}$ become virtually the same as the divisions get finer and finer, we may as well replace this sum with a different sum $\displaystyle\sum_{0\leq i,j\leq n-1} \exp(-r_i^2)\,r_i\,(r_{i+1}-r_i)(\theta_{j+1}-\theta_j)$.
It becomes clear that we are approximating $\displaystyle\int_B e^{-s^2}s\,\text{d}s\text{d}t$, where $B$ is the box on the $st$ plane defined by $[0,\sqrt{2}]\times[0,2\pi]$.
The reason people prefer the latter expression easier, is that this is equal to \[ \int_0^{2\pi}\bigg(\int_0^{\sqrt{2}}e^{-s^2}s\,\text{d}s\bigg)\text{d}t \] and this last one is very easy to give the exact value for.
I could go and demonstrate mathematically that $\displaystyle\int_B e^{-s^2}s\,\text{d}s\text{d}t$ is automatically the same as $\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}\bigg(\int_0^{\sqrt{2}}e^{-s^2}s\,\text{d}s\bigg)\text{d}t$, by carefully dividing up the $s$-axis and $t$-axis and keeping track of the fineness of the division - in a very similar manner to arguments I've already used.

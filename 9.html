The second point is this: \(\text{d}u\) is an alternative, "weird" way of measuring the size of sets inside \[0,4]\, which in turns gives us a "weird" way to measure the area under any continuous function \(f(x)\) on \(x\in[0,4]\). How so?
The normal way to measure the area is to draw the rectangles on the graph of \(f(x)\), take their bases, take their heights i.e. the values of \(f(x)\) at those division points, multiply each pair, and then add it all up. Then, use narrower and narrower divisions.
But you can imagine someone coming up with a new rule, where they say, "No! I don't want you measuring the area this way! I want you measuring the area in this other way, where every time you see a rectangle with \(x\)-axis base \([c,c']\), you pretend that the base is of length \((\sqrt{c'}-\sqrt{c})\), because that's what the new size or length of that interval is going to be. Only then, do you multiply, add, take narrower etc."
Note that we're not messing up the function \(f(x)\) itself or the positions \(x_i\) that are mapped to \(f(x_i)\). Not at all. We're messing up how big or long a set inside the \(x\)-axis is considered to be, because the bases of the rectangles are sets inside the \(x\)-axis.
Mathematically, this new rule can be applied for every continuous function \(f(x)\) on the interval \(x\in[0,4]\). It will give you a unique limit. As we discussed, the limit is \(\displaystyle\int_{u=0}^{u=2} f(u^2)\,\text{d}u\). But we can also regard this as \(\displaystyle\int_{x=0}^{x=4} f(x)\,\text{d}u\).
In other words, \(\text{d}u\) is really a measure on \([0,4]\) which is different from \(\text{d}x\). We can integrate any continuous function on \([0,4]\) with respect to these different ways of measuring sets inside \([0,4]\), and we can get different answers.
There's a whole branch of measure theory, defining what a measure is and how measures work over sets. You might even take this course in later years.

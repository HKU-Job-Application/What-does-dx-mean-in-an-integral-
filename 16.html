These shapes are inspired by polar coordinates. Presumably you've seen this in high school. To jog your memory, I have a graph to the right of this paragraph, where you can drag the point \(P\) around and observe how the coordinates change.
You've also probably seen the formula for \(x\) and \(y\) in terms of \(r\) and \(\theta\). As a reminder, it is \(x=r\cos(\theta)\) and \(y=r\sin(\theta)\).

Remember, the disc \(C\) is the set of every point on the \(xy\) plane with radius at most \(\sqrt{2}\). Therefore, in polar coordinates, we can represent \(C\) as those points where the radius lies in \([0,\sqrt{2}]\) and angle in \([0,2\pi)\).
We could "draw" the points of \(C\) on a \(r\theta\) graph, even though that isn't really where the points of \(C\) are - it's just a representation. We could imagine a \(r\)-axis as an interval from \(0\) to \(\sqrt{2}\), and a \(\theta\)-axis as an interval from \(0\) to \(2\pi\). We could then divide both axes into \(n\) equal pieces and look at the little rectangles.
Each of the new C-shapes, little pieces of rings inside \(C\), is for the actual \(xy\) graph (the lower right) what's represented by a small rectangle on the "pretend" \(r\theta\) graph (the upper right).

Very well, you might say. No surprise. We haven't even touched on the meaning of \(\text{d}x\), which is the point of the presentation.
In a sense, very roughly speaking, \(\text{d}x\) represents the tiny, in the view of calculus infinitesimally tiny, sub-lengths on the interval. We are multiplying \(f(x)\) by each tiny length, across the entire interval.
But I think that the meaning of \(\text{d}x\) comes out best, when we change the variable from \(x\) to something else and force ourselves to ask what that change does to our calculations.

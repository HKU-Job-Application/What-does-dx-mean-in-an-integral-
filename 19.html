The "proper" way of finding the volume under the surface of $f(x,y)$ over $C$ is to take each little sub-shape in $C$ based on our division, gets its actual area, multiply it by some value of $f(x,y)$ on that little shape, see that this estimates the size of a very thin part of the volume, and add up all those across all the sub-shapes that make up our division. Then, we get better estimates by making the divisions finer, the sub-shapes smaller.
Just as in previous examples, we can ask: What if we created a weird rule for estimating the integral? The new rule is that instead of taking the actual value of the area of that little piece of ring in $C$, we pretended that the sub-shape had an area equal to the sub-rectangle in our pretend $r\theta$ graph. I remind you that the $r\theta$ graph doesn't actually show the points of $C$.
As I said earlier, you could make up such a rule. Mathematically it works, and it's called having a different measure for the sizes of sets in $C$. This rule integrates $f(x,y)$ with respect to $\text{d}r\,\text{d}\theta$, a measure different from $\text{d}x\,\text{d}y$. But the areas that come out of the new measure are not the correct ones for evaluating the original integral. I've attached the $C$ and $r\theta$ graphs again - you can click or hover on any sub-shape/sub-rectangle, and an infobox shows you how different the areas are. Because the areas are not right, the estimate for the whole integral isn't right either. The infobox will also tell you so.

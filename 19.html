Notice something interesting. $\sqrt{2}\dfrac{i+\frac{1}{2}}{n}$ is exactly the average of $r_i=\sqrt{2}\dfrac{i}{n}$ and $r_{i+1}=\sqrt{2}\dfrac{i+1}{n}$. Let's rewrite the same expression.
\[ \sum_{0\leq i,j\leq n-1} f(x,y)|_{(x,y)\text{ is some point in the C-piece paired to }R_{i,j}}\,\text{Av}(r_i,r_{i+1})\,(r_{i+1}-r_i)(\theta_{j+1}-\theta_j) \]
But remember, $f(x,y)=\exp(-x^2-y^2)=\exp(-r^2)$. For $(x,y)$ corresponding to some $R_{i,j}$, the value of $x^2+y^2$ is between $r_i$ and $r_{i+1}$.
When the division gets very fine i.e. the areas very small, $r_i$ and $r_{i+1}$ become virtually the same. In the limit, you can switch out one for the other without any problems. So we're really just talking about \[ \sum_{0\leq i,j\leq n-1} \exp(-r_i^2)\,r_i\,(r_{i+1}-r_i)(\theta_{j+1}-\theta_j) \]
which in the limit we recognise as \[ \int_0^{2\pi}\bigg(\int_0^\sqrt{2}\exp(-r^2)\,r\,\text{d}r \bigg)\text{d}\theta \]
of which it's easy to give the exact value, using the substitution $u=r^2$.
